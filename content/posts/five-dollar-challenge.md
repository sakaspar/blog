Most people think budgeting is about deprivation. It's not. It's about **awareness**.

I decided to live on $5 a day for 30 days. That's $150 for the entire month. Food, transport, entertainment : everything. I wanted to see if I could survive. I wanted to see what I'd learn when the safety net was gone.

Here is the breakdown of the experiment that changed how I look at money.

---

## The Ground Rules

I kept it simple. No using existing pantry items (except salt and oil). No "invites" to dinner where someone else pays. No cheating.

- **Budget :** $5.00 per day.
- **Duration :** 30 days.
- **Goal :** Survival without losing my mind.

---

## What I ate (The Bulk Strategy)

You cannot eat out on $5 a day. Not even fast food. If you buy a $6 meal, you've already failed.

I learned to love the bulk aisle. Rice, beans, lentils, and oats became my best friends. I bought 10kg of rice on day one. It was the best investment I've ever made.

- **Oats for breakfast :** Every single day.
- **Rice and beans for lunch :** Every single day.
- **Lentil soup for dinner :** You guessed it.

It sounds boring. It was. But it was also **consistent**. I stopped thinking about what I wanted to eat and started thinking about what I needed to fuel myself.

---

## The Lessons Learned

### 1. Convenience is a tax on your time
Most of the money we spend isn't for the product : it's for the convenience. The $4 coffee. The $12 salad. The $25 Uber. When you remove convenience as an option, you realize how much of your "needs" are actually just "laziness".

### 2. Social life is expensive
My social life took a hit. "Let's grab a drink" usually means spending $15 minimum. I had to learn to say no. Or better : I had to learn to suggest free alternatives. Walking in the park is free. Talking is free.

### 3. Your perspective shifts
By day 15, a 50-cent discount on eggs felt like winning the lottery. You start to value the small things. You realize that **luxury is relative**.

---

## The Result

I survived. I actually ended the month with $4.20 left over.

But the real win wasn't the savings. It was the **discipline**. I proved to myself that I don't need "stuff" to be okay. I don't need to spend money to have a good day.

If you feel like your finances are out of control : try a radical challenge. You don't have to do $5. Try $10. Try $20.

The number doesn't matter. The **limit** does.
