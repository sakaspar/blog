Everyone wants a "transformation". Nobody wants to do the reps.

I decided to see what would happen if I did 100 push-ups every single day for 60 days. No gym. No protein shakes. No fancy equipment. Just my floor and my own body weight.

6,000 reps later, here is the result.

---

## The Protocol

I didn't do all 100 at once. At least not at the start.

- **Phase 1 (Days 1–20) :** Sets of 10–15 throughout the day.
- **Phase 2 (Days 21–40) :** Four sets of 25.
- **Phase 3 (Days 41–60) :** Two sets of 50.

The goal was consistency. Even if it was 11:30 PM and I hadn't done them, I got on the floor and did them. No excuses.

---

## The Physical Changes

I didn't turn into a bodybuilder. Let's get that out of the way. But the changes were noticeable.

1. **Shoulder definition :** My shoulders became much more capped.
2. **Tricep thickness :** The back of my arms filled out.
3. **Core stability :** A push-up is essentially a moving plank. My abs were tighter than they'd ever been.

**The "Before" :** Soft, lack of definition, struggling to do 20 reps in a row.
**The "After" :** Leaner, visible muscle lines, 50 reps feels like a warm-up.

---

## The Mental Shift (The Real Gain)

The physical stuff is nice. But the real transformation was between my ears.

Doing something every day : regardless of how you feel : builds a specific kind of mental callus. There were days I was sore. There were days I was tired. There were days I was bored.

I did it anyway.

That habit of **showing up** bled into other areas of my life. If I could do 100 push-ups when I didn't want to, I could finish that report. I could make that difficult phone call. I could stick to my budget.

---

## Should you do it?

Yes. But not for the muscles.

Do it to prove to yourself that you can stick to a commitment. Do it to build the foundation of discipline.

- **Start where you are.** (If 100 is too much, do 20).
- **Don't miss a day.**
- **Focus on form over speed.**

The floor is right there. What's your excuse? :
